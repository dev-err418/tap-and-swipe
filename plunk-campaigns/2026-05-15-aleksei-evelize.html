A backend engineer built a $17K/month app on the side

Hey, it's Arthur :)

I just published the first proper Tap & Swipe case study.

It's with Aleksei Rozhnov, a backend engineer in London. Nights and weekends, he and his childhood friend Maksim built Evelize, a teleprompter app for iOS and Android.

Last 30 days: $17K in revenue.

And the funny part is that it's not a sexy market at all. It's teleprompters. Nobody is flexing their teleprompter startup on X.

But people search for them when they need one. They compare apps. They pay if the app makes recording videos less painful. That's the whole thing.

Some notes I took from the interview:

  • Boring markets can be great. If people already search for the problem, you don't need to create demand from scratch.
  • Simple is a real advantage when the existing apps are clunky. Evelize didn't invent a new behavior. It made an annoying one easier.
  • Google Search ads are less exciting than TikTok, but the intent is already there. Someone types "teleprompter app" because they need one now.
  • They ignored Android for two years because of the "Android users don't pay" idea. Then they tested it and realized attribution was actually cleaner there.
  • $17K/month sounds like quit-your-job money until you split it between two founders, subtract ads and taxes, and compare it to London engineer salaries.

"Product is okay. Often product doesn't even matter that much. Distribution is the game."

That line hurt a little. But he's right. A beautiful app nobody sees is just a very polished secret.

The full case study gets into the messy parts: one-star paywall reviews, contradictory ad advice, why they built their own analytics instead of using RevenueCat, and why Aleksei still hasn't quit his job.
