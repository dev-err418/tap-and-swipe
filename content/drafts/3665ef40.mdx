**Mikita was a vascular surgeon. He spent 10 years making every mistake a first-time founder can make. Then he built 5 apps, sold 4 of them, including one for six figures. This is how selling apps actually works.**

---

## Who is Mikita?

<FounderCard />

Mikita is a former vascular surgeon who became a serial app founder. He had zero coding, zero business, zero product experience when he started. He saw a speed-reading technology on YouTube, got excited, and decided to build something around it. No validation, no team, no plan. Just an idea and the energy to start.

It took more than 10 years to get real results. Along the way he outsourced development with borrowed money, picked the wrong co-founders, ignored distribution, had no analytics, and added features nobody asked for. Last year, his team built 5 apps and sold 4 of them.

---

## How to validate an app idea before building it

Mikita didn't validate anything at the beginning. He thought his ideas were unique and brilliant. They weren't.

His approach now is completely different. Before building, he focuses on two things: does a real user pain exist, and how will he acquire users for the product.

> You need to focus on distribution from day one. Before you build anything, you need to understand how you will acquire users. If you don't know how, you need to understand who can acquire them for you.

He uses Sensor Tower, TrustMRR, and X to research markets. The metric he cares about most is average revenue per user (ARPU). If competitors are getting $2-3 per user, he knows paid acquisition can work at higher price points later. He also checks the Meta ad library and TikTok ads to understand which channels competitors use to acquire users.

## How to build an app without coding experience

Mikita started with paper sketches, YouTube tutorials on UI/UX, and Photoshop mockups. This was 10 years ago, before Figma existed. He found a non-technical co-founder, they borrowed money from their families, and hired an outsourcing team.

The outsourcing team delivered exactly what was in the design. Nothing more. No product thinking, no growth thinking, just execution. That's the trap with outsourcing: you get what you ask for, not what you need.

Today he uses AI for two things. First, continuing development on existing apps by feeding the design system to Claude. Second, prototyping new apps from scratch using Figma Make or tools that generate design and code simultaneously.

His take on non-technical founders building with AI: you can validate fast, but to build something scalable you probably still need a technical co-founder. AI doesn't replace judgment.

> To build something good with AI, you need knowledge. It's very difficult to have zero knowledge and build something valuable.

## How to market a mobile app with no budget

This was Mikita's biggest mistake. He thought a good product would sell itself. It didn't.

> I always focused on product. All mentors told me to focus on product, retention, unit economics. Very few talked about distribution. And in my case, not starting paid acquisition was the biggest mistake, because 10 years ago ads were crazy cheap.

He tried working with publishing agencies on revenue-share deals. It never worked well. The agencies weren't invested in the results, and without initial traction, they had nothing predictable to scale.

What actually worked: cold outreach to an ASO agency founder. He reached out, proposed a revenue share, and she said yes. One cold message, one partnership, and things finally started moving.

> Everything is open right now. LinkedIn, X, you can write to anybody. Usually when you have little experience, you're scared to ask someone for help because you're not confident in your product. But you can ask. And one good partner can change everything faster than months of building.

## How to use app analytics to make better decisions

For years, Mikita only looked at installs and revenue. No funnels, no retention, no idea what users actually did inside the app.

Now he tracks everything in layers. App Store metrics first: impressions to install conversion. This tells you if your icon, screenshots, title, and subtitle are working. You have less than a second to catch someone's attention as they scroll.

> Title, subtitle, icon, and first three screenshots. The user needs to understand what your app is about in one single moment.

Then inside the app: onboarding completion (and which screen users drop off on), paywall conversion, and which subscription plan gets purchased. But he warns against optimizing blindly. Improving onboarding completion can actually hurt paywall conversion if you're letting the wrong users through. You need to test constantly and look at the full funnel, not just one metric.

## How to sell a mobile app and how much you can get

One day Mikita received an email. Someone wanted to buy one of his apps. The offer: **24x MRR**. He was shocked.

They closed the deal in two weeks. The app sold for **six figures**.

After that first success, he sold three more apps. In total, 4 apps sold.

What buyers care about:

**Unit economics.** They need to understand how the app will perform in the future. Revenue, costs, margins. It needs to be predictable.

**Organic sustainability.** The best scenario for a buyer is an app that can live without paid acquisition. If you stopped marketing one year before the sale and the app still generates revenue, the multiplier goes up. 24x MRR can become 36x or more if the app proves it can survive on its own.

**What you have to transfer.** It depends on the buyer. Some just want the code and the app transfer. Others want everything: code, Figma files, all App Store metadata, privacy policy, terms of service, ad accounts. Read the agreement carefully.

**Non-compete clause.** His first contract included a clause that he couldn't develop similar apps for one year. This is common and easy to overlook. Read every point in the contract because it can limit you after the sale.

> Before I sold my first app, I didn't even know it was possible. After the first deal, I understood how the market works and what buyers look for. Then I sold three more.

---

## When is the right time to sell an app

Mikita's answer: it depends on your goals. If you understand how to build and scale apps, the money from a sale can fund your next projects. If you're stuck and can't grow the MRR further, selling might be the right move.

He thinks **$2K MRR** is the minimum where you can expect a reasonable deal. Below that, it's hard to find serious buyers.

The best time to sell is when the app can demonstrate organic revenue without paid acquisition. Stop running ads a few months before you plan to sell. If the revenue holds, your multiplier goes up significantly.
