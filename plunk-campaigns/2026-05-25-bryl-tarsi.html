He built a paid app in a weekend. It made $20K in month one.

Hey, it's Arthur :)

I just published a new Tap & Swipe case study, and this one made me rethink something I probably say too confidently.

It's with Bryl Lim, the creator of Tarsi, a budget tracking app that started as a demo for a university talk about vibe coding.

He built it in a weekend, published it as a paid app, and then people started buying it.

First month: roughly $20K in revenue.

No paid ads. No freemium funnel. No subscription wall before you can use the actual app.

Which sounds wrong, honestly. Paid apps are supposed to be dead. Everyone is supposed to build free apps, add a paywall, run ads, optimize onboarding, and slowly lose their mind in a dashboard somewhere.

Bryl basically skipped all of that.

He charged upfront, brought traffic from a public Facebook group, asked users for reviews inside the app, and let the paid charts do their thing once the store had a signal.

Some notes I took from the interview:

  • Paid app charts are weirdly underused. Everyone is fighting in free apps, while paid charts need far fewer downloads to rank.
  • A paid upfront app can feel refreshing now. People are tired of downloading something free and discovering three screens later that the app was never really free.
  • Tarsi gets around 80-90 downloads a day, which is enough to sit at #1 on paid charts in multiple countries.
  • The Facebook group is not just support. When users post or comment, their friends see it, get curious, and some of them buy the app.
  • But this only works because he brought external traffic first. ASO doesn't magically wake up for a new paid app. The Facebook group created movement, then in-app reviews and chart momentum helped the stores rank him higher.
  • Keeping the core app offline means almost no operating costs. Every paid download is much closer to profit.

"If you think your app looks good, you've probably shipped too late."

That one is painfully useful. Especially if you're the kind of person who keeps polishing the same screen because you're secretly afraid of finding out nobody wants it.
